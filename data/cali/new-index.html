Californian Conservation

Avriana Allen

Medill | 3/17/2019

During the first few months of this year, Californiaâ€™s reservoirs were replenished with around 580 billion gallons of water and its snowpack doubled twice, two years after former Gov. Jerry Brown declared California was no longer in a state of emergency due to drought.

Yet the question remains: what should California do with its water?

For nearly two decades, the state has rarely been completely drought-free.

To combat this, the most populous state in the US ran a series of radio and television advertisements encouraging residents to lower their water consumption through shorter showers, eco-friendly lawns and water-saving appliances.

With more than 70% of the population served by a public supply system, reducing consumption was priority, even leading to rebates for effective equipment.

Though there are a few counties that fall below the 70% line, most are within the range.

Public supply is drawn from two sources: groundwater and surface water.

Groundwater accumulates underground and is usually accessed by using wells or pumps. Surface water, in contrast is defined as all exposed bodies of water including lakes, rivers, streams and shallow wells.

There is, however, a distinct geographical element associated with the populations served.

The yearly data suggests that California relies heavily on groundwater during droughts, but in wetter years, such as 2005, surface water makes a greater contribution.

However, despite numerous advertisements about reducing water, the numbers for public water supply continued to rise with the population, averaging in 2010 at 35 billion gallons a day.

In other words, the 580 billion gallons accumulated over the winter of 2019, would be gone in about 16.5 days.

Despite these watery struggles, California is also an agricultural state.

With a wide variety of produce, it grows everything from alfalfa to grapes.

However, the only crop with a steady downward trend in water use is sugar beets.

This is unfortunate, given the effective rain for crops each year.

According to the California Department of Water Resources, the Californian climate is susceptible to drought, which means that it could be another few years before California receives rain like it did this winter.

Therefore, given the high numbers of human consumption, and the even higher numbers in relation to agriculture, it is as important as ever to continue to find ways to conserve water in the recently rainy sunshine state.
